Negative vs Positive Ions

Understanding the sources and effects of natural and electronic ion energy.

What Are Ions?

An ion is an atom or molecule that has gained or lost one or more electrons, leaving it with a net electric charge.[1]

  • Negative ions (anions): Have extra electrons and a negative charge.
  • Positive ions (cations): Have lost electrons and a positive charge.

Where They Come From

Negative and positive ions form naturally and artificially:

  • Negative ions: Created by natural forces—lightning, waterfalls, sunlight, ocean waves, and moving air— as energetic collisions free electrons that quickly attach to passing molecules.[2]
  • Positive ions: Often produced by electronic devices, especially CRT monitors, air conditioning, pollution, and synthetic materials, where electrical fields and heat strip electrons from particles and leave a positively charged residue linked with headaches and low mood in sensitive people.[4]

Do Screens Emit Positive Ions?

Flat-panel displays, laptops, and phone chargers operate at voltages that are far below the sharp, kilovolt potentials required to ionize air. Measurements in offices and hospitals show that switching these devices on and off keeps positive ion counts within normal indoor ranges (hundreds per cubic centimeter), with ventilation and humidity driving most of the variation instead.[6][7]

Laboratory work on "spark-less" electrostatic discharge confirms that glass touchscreens primarily hold surface charge instead of releasing clouds of free positive ions into the room air.[8]

When people report headaches, eye strain, or fatigue around screens, the culprits are typically flicker, glare, blue light, or simply long periods without breaks—photobiological stressors that affect circadian rhythm and visual comfort rather than ion chemistry.[9]

Scientific & Reference Sources

Explore the studies and expert explainers referenced in this overview:

  • Natural negative ions can reach tens of thousands per cubic centimeter near waterfalls and surf zones according to atmospheric measurements summarized by the NIH / NCBI review.[2]
  • The PubMed survey catalogues laboratory and field experiments linking ion polarity with respiratory and mood outcomes.[3]
  • Scientific American reports that poorly designed ionizers can emit ozone and elevate positive-ion counts, aggravating asthma in sensitive populations.[5]
  • The University of Alaska's Geophysical Institute summarizes research showing how CRT-equipped offices deplete negative ions and how restoring them reduces headaches and other complaints.[4]

Health Insights from the Research

  • The NIH / NCBI review summarizes clinical trials where exposure to excess positive ions elevated serotonin and migraine symptoms, while negative ion generators helped restore balance.[2]
  • The PubMed survey reports that polluted indoor spaces rich in positive ions are linked with higher respiratory irritation, cardiovascular stress markers, and mood disturbances compared with negative-ion balanced environments.[3]
  • Scientific American notes that some ionizer designs emit ozone and large amounts of positive ions, aggravating asthma and causing headaches in sensitive individuals.[5]
  • Geophysical Institute reporting describes offices where positive static charge from CRTs and air conditioning reduce negative ions, correlating the drop with headaches, dizziness, and nausea until ion levels are restored.[4]

In Simple Terms

Energy in nature tends to produce negative ions, while modern electronics and pollution tend to produce positive ions. Balancing the two—through grounding, fresh air, and mindful design—can help maintain well-being and clarity.
